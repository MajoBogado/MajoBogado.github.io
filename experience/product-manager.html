Product Manager

I've been in this role for almost two years but honestly, I'd been doing parts of it for a while already. The move from product design to Product Management felt like a natural next step.

If I had to describe where I add the most value:

I'm good at 0-to-1. When there's nothing yet, no definition, no direction. I know how to go out and find the answers, organize priorities, shape that first version of a product, and make sure everyone involved (stakeholders and delivery teams alike) is actually aligned before we build anything.

I'm comfortable with ambiguity. Not just tolerating it, but working through it.

I can sit with engineering, understand the complexity of what's being asked, make sense of estimates, and spot where something can be simplified without compromising what actually matters.

I never lose sight of the user. Having a design background means the user experience is always in the room with me, in every prioritization call, every tradeoff, every review.

I bridge design and engineering without letting the user experience get lost in translation. It happens all the time: something gets designed carefully, thoughtfully, and then by the time it hits implementation (whether it's a timeline crunch, a handoff gap, or someone new picking it up) it comes out different. I'm the one making sure the final product doesn't quietly compromise what was designed to matter.

I also work closely with engineering on the technical side, proposing and scoping POCs before committing to full development, identifying potential blockers early, and making sure that implementations (especially around data) are correct from an auditability, consistency, and scalability standpoint. It might not be visible today, but I'm thinking about what this product needs to be six months from now.

I keep an eye on the process itself. If something in how the team is working isn't efficient, I'll flag it and propose a fix.

I'm also good at building strong relationships with stakeholders, and I do it through structure, communication, and transparency. In my experience, most people are flexible: when they understand how you work and what to expect, a lot of the friction just dissolves on its own. That trust doesn't happen by accident; it has to be built deliberately.

Product management, at its best, is invisible. Things just work, teams stay aligned, and the product ships in a way that makes sense. That's what I'm here for.
