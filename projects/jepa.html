JEPA

Disclaimer: A lot of what follows is my own conjecture — it's how I got to JEPA, and it reflects my broader approach to AI given its current paradigm.

Trying to understand how Artificial Intelligence works started with a more fundamental question: what is intelligence?

When I began reading about AI (late, I'll admit) I found myself trying to pin down a definition for Intellicence, and discovered it's surprisingly slippery. A bit like asking someone to define art: there are many definitions, everyone picks their own, and I have mine. But with intelligence, I had to actually walk that path. I still don't have a definition that fully convinces me.

We talk about different "types" of intelligence, and what we're trying to do, at minimum, is replicate the human kind (hence the word artificial). But that opens another can of worms, because not all humans think, behave, or act the same way. So how do we define what counts as intelligent? If you sit with that question for a moment, you start to realize that some of the conversations we're having around current AI are exactly that: what would it need to do for me to consider it intelligent? And that's where it gets complicated again, because the answer depends on the person, the task, the specific interaction, and the expectations they bring to it.

As I mentioned elsewhere, my first real engagement with this was an article I wrote back in 2022. Even then I had started questioning the current AI paradigm, not because something was fundamentally wrong with it, but because of its obvious limitations. Limitations we're now actively trying to work around, which is why terms like "harnessing", "guardrails", "memory", and "context" keep appearing, they're essentially patches. And we've come a long way since November 2022. The models have improved significantly. But now we're in a moment of trying to extract maximum value from what we've built, because it was expensive to build and it's expensive to adopt, and both costs sit on the same side of the equation.

I have a lot more to say about AI, but this section would never end. The point is: in trying to understand how AI works in its current paradigm, I started wondering whether anyone was working on different paradigms and what they were finding.

Two things stood out:

  • There's a line of work I find fascinating (though I'm not sure how far it will go) that could be described as biomimetic: trying to generate artificial intelligence by replicating the mechanisms that produce human intelligence. The catch is we don't actually know what those mechanisms are. There are theories, hypotheses, people working on them, but it remains one of the great unsolved mysteries.
  • There's another line of work that takes a different approach: keeping what's good about current AI, but trying to address its problems not by adding layers on top of existing models, but by changing how models are trained and how they predict.

That second one is JEPA. And to try to understand how it works and what makes it different, I've been doing exercises to explore its potential use cases.

So far I've done two things:

  1. An initial exploratory exercise: a hands-on comparison of three fundamentally different approaches to longitudinal patient outcome prediction, built to understand how trajectory-based architectures (like JEPA) differ structurally from language models (LLMs) and traditional machine learning (like XGBoost). View on GitHub.
  2. As part of the final deliverable for Module 3 of my MSc in AI in Healthcare, I proposed going one step further and incorporating imaging data into the analysis. This proposal is currently theoretical, but I'd love to implement it when I find the time.

And that, simply put, is what this project is about: exploring different approaches to building Artificial Intelligence and different use cases for each because maybe that is another way to proceed here, understanding which model is best for which case.
