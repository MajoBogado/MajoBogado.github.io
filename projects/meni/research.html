Research

The image above is, in a way, a summary of all the research done during my Interactive Design master's at UBA.

Put like that, it looks like a neat, orderly process. It wasn't. It was chaotic (in the best way)

As I mentioned somewhere earlier, I knew the topic I wanted to work with, but I had no idea what shape it would take or where it would land.

Methodologically, I combined two theoretical frameworks with a people-centered approach:

  1. Erika Hall, 2019 — Just Enough Research: helped me define the broad stages that guided my research, with each stage building on the results of the previous one.
  2. Bon Ku and Ellen Lupton, 2022 — Health Design Thinking: helped me structure each stage internally at a more granular level — defining not just what methods and tools to use, but when and why at each step.

The starting point was the general topic: vertigo, dizziness, instability. People living with these symptoms. Conversations with them and with the healthcare professionals who work with them (when these people actually make it to a specialist, because that is also part of the problem).

Those conversations let me see what was hiding beneath the surface. They also made it clear that the problem space was too complex to tackle in full within the scope of a master's thesis, so I made a necessary cut based on the information I collected so far: my work would focus on people with Vestibular Disorders within the context of Vestibular Rehabilitation.

As part of this research project:

  • I spoke with people living with vestibular disorders, the professionals treating them, and their families and caregivers.
  • I prototyped and built devices that would later be used during Vestibular Rehabilitation sessions with patients.
  • I designed a visit and data collection protocol to work with patients during their sessions.
  • I analyzed the collected data.
  • I went back to the question of what the solution actually was, drawing not just from the hard objective data from the device, but from all those conversations and interactions with patients.
  • I spoke with colleagues working in design and health at companies like Philips and institutions like the Dyson School of Design Engineering at Imperial College London, which helped broaden my perspective, from methodological approach to questions like how to evaluate the impact of a proposed solution.
  • Finally, I presented a design proposal that included the full identity design for the project and app designs for three types of users: patient, referring physician, and specialist physiotherapist in Vestibular Rehabilitation.

Meni is looking for its place outside the academic world. If you didn't know this already: building a startup in healthcare is incredibly complex. Meni has been trying to find its way for a few years now, and it's going to happen in stages.
